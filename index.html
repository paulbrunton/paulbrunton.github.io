About

Paul Brunton is a contemporary New Zealand sculptor whose work explores the intersection of cultural identity, environmental concerns, and the geological history of Aotearoa New Zealand. Working primarily with stone, bronze, wood, and found materials, Brunton creates pieces that reflect both the natural landscape and the cultural narratives of his homeland.

Born in Wellington in 1975, Brunton studied at Elam School of Fine Arts at the University of Auckland, graduating with honors in 1998. His early works showed a strong interest in Māori and Pacific artistic traditions, which he has continued to incorporate into his practice, always with deep respect and cultural sensitivity.

Brunton's sculptures often feature a distinctive combination of polished and rough-hewn surfaces, creating a dialogue between the refined and the raw. His use of native materials—pounamu (New Zealand jade), kauri wood, and local stone—connects his work to the specific geography and cultural context of New Zealand while addressing universal themes of human relationship to place and history.

His sculptures are held in major public and private collections throughout New Zealand, Australia, and internationally. Brunton currently works from his studio in the Waitakere Ranges near Auckland, where the dramatic landscape directly influences his artistic practice.

Studio

Situated in the lush Waitakere Ranges west of Auckland, Paul Brunton's studio is a converted timber barn that provides both indoor and outdoor workspace for his sculptural practice. The studio environment reflects Brunton's dedication to sustainable practices, with natural lighting, rainwater collection systems, and solar power.

The surrounding native bush and proximity to the rugged west coast beaches provide constant inspiration for his work, with many of his materials sourced from the local environment in collaboration with conservation authorities.
